Collaborative leaders can build on this vision of something radically different

Wednesday February 6, 2013

The collaborative leaders today are breaking barriers of hierarchal leadership practices that are leading to creation of an open and empowered culture, within your organization, networks, and communities. My article, Learning Your Collaborative Leadership Style and Empowering Others provides several insights from leaders like Marc Benioff, CEO of Salesforce.com and Bill George, former CEO of Medtronic.

I believe the leaders who will guide organizations into something radically different have a combination of talents, not only an openly collaborative style, but also have the qualities George discovered -- leading to serve others.

Don Tapscott, futurist and author of several compelling books has presented a challenge for future leaders to create something radically different. In Tapscott's Ted Talk, titled Four Principles for the Open World he steps through an inspiring comparison to starling bird populations in Edinburgh, Scotland that have this kind of collective power, to present this challenge:

"Can we go beyond sharing information and knowledge -- and can we share our intelligence to create some kind of collective intelligence  -- to create some kind of consciousness on a global basis?" Tapscott asks.

My first thoughts in response to Tapscott's challenge is that we need to define a common vocabulary for global consciousness, like global mindfulness. Our net generation as Tapscott refers to the Gen Y's, are looking for opportunities to grow pesticide free crops, build energy efficient vehicles, and develop health regimes so we can lead healthier lives.

But the solution does not lie ahead with one person, one organization, or one generation. Like the starlings, working toward common goals, we can be building on this vision of something radically different for our human race. My next suggestion for our global consciousness is that we need to be part of a solution, not a problem.

Moneyball teaches us how to play the game with talent data

Thursday January 31, 2013

Nominated for six Academy Awards in 2012, including Best Motion Picture, Moneyball is a lesson not in winning, but how we play the game.

Brad Pitt plays the general manager of the Oakland A's who struggled to get a winning team after many years under financial hardship. In his role, Pitt decides to hire an assistant, played by Jonah Hill, just out of college, with statistical data management abilities, who says:

"People are overlooked for a variety of biased reasons and perceived flaws: age, appearance, personality. Of the 20,000 notable players for us to consider, I believe there is a championship team of 25 people that we can afford, because everyone else in baseball undervalues them."

A new Salesforce app called Talent Analytics is a lot like Moneyball. Talent Analytics data is one way to have better insights about people on your team -- how to identify and work with great collaborators, closers, and relationship builders.

Find out more about Talent Analytics for Salesforce. Throw out the old narratives and start fresh. The Talent Analytics playbook is for sales and customer support teams, so you can better understand how to relate to your individual team members. Then, think about how to play the game.

Driving Questions for Group Collaborators

Tuesday January 29, 2013

Speed and performance are important factors to accelerate results for your group or client teams. What needs to happen to move your projects along and to achieve the outcomes you've intended?

Too often group collaborators are faced with road blocks at the start without leadership visibility, team organization, and performance goals.

To help you move beyond the road blocks, take a look at several collective actions you can direct through basic questions and illustrated examples - to achieve bolder and more effective collaboration results.  It's a lot easier when you have trust and can state your honest intentions.

And can your organization solve challenges of virtual team distance, in terms of relationships and cultural differences? Find out more about virtual distance, and alternatives you may want to consider.

Regaining your creative confidence to help generate more ideas and make better decisions

Thursday January 24, 2013

In eleven minutes, David Kelley, founder of IDEO, and professor of design thinking at Stanford University, offers you a unique challenge -- to regain your creative confidence. At a recent TED talk, he shared a powerful observation:

"Opting out [of creativity] that happens in childhood...moves in and becomes more ingrained by the time you get to adult life."

In the presentation, Kelley describes a heartfelt tale he experienced in childhood.

Kelley also provides an example of an MRI equipment designer who came up with a breakthrough design to help children overcome fears of this diagnostic procedure, as well as the designer's own personal transformation.

To help you face obstacles in developing innovation programs, check out my collection for your innovation toolkit for developing innovators. Keep in mind innovators are not necessarily creative types.

Kelley's mission is an inspiring moment and a quest  --  to help as many people regain the creative confidence they lost along the way.
