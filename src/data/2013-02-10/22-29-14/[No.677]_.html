Paterno Family: Freeh Report Was 'Fundamentally Flawed'

Sunday February 10, 2013

More than a year after his passing, the family of the late Joe Paterno has officially launched their effort to clear his name.

On Sunday morning, the Paterno family released a new report that they say calls into question the legitimacy and accuracy of the controversial Freeh report, which concluded that Penn State's top leaders, including Paterno, failed badly in their responsibility to report what they knew about Jerry Sandusky's abuse of children.

That report formed the basis for the NCAA's historic and unprecedented punishment of the Penn State football program and went a long way toward tarnishing Paterno's legacy and reputation.

But the Paterno report makes the claim that Freeh overreached with his report, arrived at conclusions without having any evidence to back them up and unfairly targeted both Paterno and the Penn State football culture. The men behind the Paterno report (who, it should be noted, were hired by the Paterno family) concluded that the Freeh report was "factually wrong, speculative and fundamentally flawed."

"The Freeh report is a profound failure," said Wick Sollers, the Paterno family attorney. "It isn't a little wrong on the minor issues. It is totally wrong on the most critical issues. That the Board and the NCAA relied on this report, without appropriate review or analysis, is a miscarriage of justice."

Former Attorney General Dick Thornburgh, one of the men behind the investigation, added that he believed that, in the case of Paterno, the Freeh report amounted to "a rush to injustice."

Freeh, for his part, defended his work, and called the Paterno report "self-serving."

National Signing Day Recap: Ole Miss Makes a Charge, Penn State Thrives Despite Scandal, Northern Powers Make a Stand

Wednesday February 6, 2013

It's a very tired cliche at this point to say that National Signing Day has become an unofficial holiday for college football fans.

But the thing is, there is a lot of truth in that cliche. Because in many corners of this college football nation, National Signing Day really is a holiday. Here in the doldrums of winter, with the next season so far away and even those spring games months ahead in the distance, National Signing Day offers more than just something to talk about.

It also offers us hope.

That's especially true this year if you're an Ole Miss fan, or a Penn State fan, or a fan of any those schools that so routinely and so predictably end up among the Top 10 of the recruiting ranking (but, yeah, especially if you're an Ole Miss fan).

Yes, it may be based entirely on speculation, and yes, those "star rankings" are completely subjective.

But National Signing Day really does matter. It matters a lot.

2013 Season Preview: Is There Any Way Back for the Big Ten?

Thursday January 31, 2013

I'll admit this right up front: I'm a Big Ten guy.

I grew up in Ohio, where I was raised on the Gospel of Woody and Bo, and I went to school at Penn State, where in the 1990s I saw some truly great Big Ten teams, some truly great Big Ten games, and some truly great Big Ten players.

I saw Orlando Pace and Eddie George at Ohio State, Desmond Howard and Charles Woodson at Michigan and LaVar Arrington and Ki-Jana Carter at Penn State. I saw Ron Dayne at Wisconsin, Drew Brees at Purdue and Antwaan Randel-El at Indiana.

I saw, in other words, the Big Ten at its best.

But today, I'm seeing ... well, I'm seeing the Big Ten at its worst.

These are dark, dark times for the once proud Big Ten, and the question everybody seems to asking is this: Is there any way forward for this league?

That's the question I attempt to answer here, as I begin to look ahead to the 2013 Big Ten football season, including an up-close look at Penn State. More team-specific previews to come, folks, so stay tuned.
