Dreaming of an Adventure by Disney

Sunday February 10, 2013

When traveling with grandchildren, Grandpa and I usually opt to save money and either tent camp or cabin camp. As I get older, however, I'm more attracted to hassle-free travel. Adventures by Disney, one of several companies specializing in multigenerational travel, has three trips planned for the summer that are especially suitable for groups including grandparents.

  • Viva Italia offers a gondola ride, a pasta-making lesson at a Tuscan estate and instruction in Venetian mask-making.
  • The Cities of Knights and Lights tour includes a double-decker bus tour of London, a night in an English manor house, the Eiffel Tower and a bike ride through the gardens at Versailles.
  • The Emerald Isle tour offers a stay in a castle, a scone-making lesson at a sheep farm and a bike tour of Killarney National Park.

The Adventures by Disney website is super helpful. Click on a destination, and three tabs appear: Trip At-a-Glance; Rates and Dates; and Trip Tips & FAQs. You can learn about topics such as weather, currency and what to pack, all without leaving the website.

I may not be able to swap my cabin for a castle this year, but, hey, a girl can dream.

Which Disney adventure would be your dream trip?

Tape Today, Bubble Wrap Tomorrow?

Saturday February 9, 2013

Yesterday's Dear Abby featured a letter from Well-Meaning Grandpa, who doesn't like what happens to his house when his 9-month-old grandson visits. Drawers and cabinets must be taped shut and furniture moved to conceal electrical cords.

Childproofing a grandparent's house is a tricky issue. The object should be to make it as safe as possible for the grandchild without putting too great a burden on grandparents. Also, everyone should realize that no home is truly childproof and should concentrate instead on watching the grandchild closely. Most grandparents will be happy to volunteer for that assignment.

Grandpa does go over the line, however, when he asks: "Am I unreasonable to think my grandson is capable of learning limits with a simple 'no-no' and, perhaps, a little smack on his hand?" No grandparent should suggest striking a child, even a "little smack." Making that suggestion clearly crosses from grandparenting territory into parenting territory. Grandparents must understand the parenting style being used with grandchildren and also respect boundaries.

I do not, however, agree with Abby's solution, which is to visit the grandson in his own home. No way would I be willing to give up the pleasure --and the convenience -- of seeing my grandchildren in my own home, not even if I am required to bubble-wrap the entire premises.

Do you have a solution to Grandpa's dilemma? Leave a comment below.

Goodbye to the Iron, and Other Monopoly Mutations

Wednesday February 6, 2013

True family story: When my first granddaughter was a preschooler, she saw my iron up on a shelf and asked, "What's that?"

Before you cast aspersions on my daughter's housekeeping habits, I must admit that my granddaughter had spent many hours at my house without ever having encountered said iron. I suppose the non-ironer didn't fall too far from the tree.

I was meditating on the iron because, after a close race with the wheelbarrow and the shoe, the iron is being booted from the roster of Monopoly tokens. The thimble, arguably more obsolete than the iron, was evidently never in danger. Everyone's having a lot of fun with the iron's ouster, but NPR scooped everyone by scoring an interview with the iron.

I was never a big fan of Monopoly, although it was a part of my childhood, as it must have been for most 50s children. Monopoly Junior, which is one of my favorite games to play with the grandchildren, is lots more fun to me than the traditional game, which simply takes too long to play, and always did. I've not tried the Wii or iPad versions, and the idea of Spongebob SquarePants Monopoly leaves me cold.  That must mean I'm a bit of a traditionalist. Still, I'm not mourning the loss of the iron token. And my own iron still mostly stays on the shelf.

Grand Photo of the Week: Happy New Year

Wednesday February 6, 2013

The Chinese New Year, that is. The 15-day celebration that begins on Feb. 10 offers lots of opportunities for fun with the grandchildren. Teach them to prepare some traditional foods. Give a gift of  money in the traditional red envelopes. Make paper lanterns for the final celebration, Lantern Day. And don't forget to take lots of photographs. The New Year is also a time to visit with family and to mend any damaged relationships. That's a tradition that perhaps all families should adopt.
