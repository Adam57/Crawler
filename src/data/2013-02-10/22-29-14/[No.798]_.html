Greed Kills

Friday February 8, 2013

Reaching for yield is an old story within investing, and is the underpinning of many sad stories about financial scams and financial crashes. Unfortunately, retail investors aren't the only ones who fall into this trap. Desperate professional investment managers also are prone to make this mistake, with wide reaching ramifications both for their own clients and for the financial system as a whole. Read on for a summary of recent academic research into this matter.

A related issue involves the travails of insurance companies that offer fixed annuities, spurring them to sell these contracts to more aggressive money managers.

Whether you are a personal investor or a professional money manager or risk manager, learn to recognize the signs and the implications of undue reaching for yield.

Outsourcing Your Own Job

Wednesday February 6, 2013

Shortly after we advised you about employee surveillance systems, a fascinating news story broke about an enterprising computer programmer who outsourced his own job. Maybe you've caught it, but in case you didn't, here are the essentials.

The guy worked for Verizon Communications and found a Chinese firm that would do his work for about 20% of what he was being paid. So, he outsourced his programming assignments to that firm, pocketed the difference, and spent his days at the office surfing the web. Problem is, he didn't think that Verizon was monitoring his computer usage. Game over.

Call this a unique variety of arbitrage.

Financial Representatives

Monday February 4, 2013

Among the often confusing proliferation of new job titles within financial services firms is that of financial representative. Generally speaking, it denotes a species of insurance sales agent who also is involved with investments and/or financial planning. If nothing else, the title is intended to confer a great deal more cachet than the former, evoking the world of the financial advisor in the process.

Financial Con Artists and Their Marks

Friday February 1, 2013

A professor of law, who also specializes in financial markets and regulation, has completed an exhaustive study of financial scam victims, looking for the most common traits that characterize them. An ancillary set of findings indicate what are the traits of the most effective financial con artists, and they may surprise you.

Whatever your career path within finance, these findings are worth a look. Indeed, they may be important from the standpoint of improving your self-knowledge and making yourself less susceptible to getting conned. And don't think that you're too smart or too skeptical to fall for these tricks. The professor finds that some otherwise extremely bright people are among the most common victims.
