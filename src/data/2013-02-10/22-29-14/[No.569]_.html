Confusing Credit Union Eligibility

Friday February 8, 2013

Have you ever tried to join a credit union and been denied? Don't take it personally. Credit unions are required to limit their customers (or "members") to a specific group of people. In some cases, that group is defined as:

  • Employees at a certain company
  • Residents of a community or town
  • Members of an organization

If you're not part of a group that the credit union works with, the credit union can't work with you -- the institution will get in trouble with its regulators and can even be shut down if it opens accounts for outsiders.

Ken Tumin explains in more detail how a credit union's field of membership works, and how membership rules are sometimes confusing.

If you're having a hard time finding a credit union that you're eligible to join, consider joining one that's open to almost anybody (there's a list at DepositAccounts.com). You should also ask if your employer has a relationship with any local credit unions.

Online Payday Loans Riskier than Usual

Wednesday February 6, 2013

Payday loans are almost never a good idea. They're so expensive that it's difficult to pay them off, and they often end up costing more than borrowers ever imagined.

What could possibly make things worse? Getting scammed instead of getting an expensive loan. That's exactly what happens to too many hopeful borrowers who apply for payday loans online. They find that thieves have set up fake websites offering loans, but the money never comes. Instead, borrowers are asked to pay fees before the loan can be processed (sometimes via wire transfer, which means your money is gone for good).

If you absolutely have to use a payday loan, it's probably safer to do it in person. A brick-and-mortar shop is more likely to be legitimate than a website; the store owner had to lease the space, put up signs, decorate, and so on, which suggests that they intend to make money and get repeat customers -- but anybody can create a fly-by-night website in a few minutes.

Better yet, try to avoid payday loans altogether. Consider selling things you own, peer to peer loans, or visiting your bank or credit union to find out about all of the ways you can borrow money.

Credit Card Surcharges Allowed, but Unlikely

Thursday January 31, 2013

You may want to pay attention the next time you make a purchase with plastic. Keep an eye out for extra charges that are supposed to cover retailers' costs of accepting credit cards.

Retailers in most states recently got the green light to pass those costs on to customers. The charge is usually 1% to 4% of your purchase amount.

However, you may find that few retailers are willing to take this step. They're worried that it will look like they're nickel-and-diming you, and that never feels good -- even if it's a cost you'd pay anyway. You already pay the fees (in the form of inflated prices) so there's little reason for them to rock the boat. However, some businesses may choose to cut prices across the board and offer a discount to customers who are less expensive to work with -- those who pay cash.

If you find that retailers are charging an extra fee, make sure they're not double dipping. Ask if they've lowered their prices as a result of the new rules before you pay extra.

Fannie and Freddie to Approve Deed-in-Lieu Mortgage Help

Tuesday January 29, 2013

If your home is worth less than you owe on it, you may soon be able to hand your deed over to the bank and avoid making future payments on your home. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac announced that they will start offering these deed-in-lieu transactions to some borrowers starting in March.

To qualify for the transaction, you have to be current on your mortgage and keep your home in good condition. This program will not be available to borrowers who have stopped making payments or abandoned their homes. If approved, you won't have to pay back the difference between your home's value and your loan balance, but your credit will suffer some.

Some say that this move will help reduce foreclosures and benefit everybody -- homeowners, their neighbors, and lenders. Others argue that it will increase pressure on taxpayer-funded agencies that are already strained, and that the recovering housing market makes this gesture unnecessary.

If you have an underwater mortgage, keep making those payments until you find out what all the options are. You may be able to use this program, but there are other options available that can help you keep your home (such as HARP 2.0).
