Women's World Cup: Super Sixes Preview

Wednesday February 6, 2013
Charlotte Edwards
www.flickr.com user paddynapper

The shock news from the Women's Cricket World Cup 2013 (WWC13) is that India, the host nation, has failed to qualify for the Super Sixes stage after finishing bottom of their group. They ended level on points with West Indies, whose enormous win over Sri Lanka saw them edge India out with a superior net run rate.

Apart from that, things have gone more or less as expected. Australia and England have topped the two groups. Past winners New Zealand are through, as are up-and-comers Sri Lanka, West Indies, and South Africa. England captain Charlotte Edwards is scoring lots of runs. Katherine Brunt, the fiercest fast bowler in women's cricket, is topping the bowling tables.

The surprise package has been Sri Lanka. They came into the tournament unheralded and were expected to finish bottom of Group A, but outstanding and unprecedented wins over England and India saw them qualify second in their group. That means they carry two valuable points forward to the Super Sixes -- but they'll need a lot more wins to reach the final, given their poor net run rate.

The next Women's World Cup match will be the playoff for 7th and 8th place between neighbouring rivals India and Pakistan. After that, the Super Sixes get underway with what is sure to be a close, exciting contest between Australia and England. Australia will have one foot in the final if they win, while England need to keep bouncing back from that first-up loss to Sri Lanka. Whatever the result, there's a good chance these two teams will contest the WWC13 final on February 17.

Women's World Cup Off With A Bang

Thursday January 31, 2013

After the opening match of the Women's Cricket World Cup 2013 (WWC13), it seems Deandra Dottin may be the female cricketing world's first big-hitting superstar.

Women's cricket traditionally brings to mind a more technical style of play, as women are not generally as powerfully built as men and therefore rely more on technique and timing to hit fours and sixes. Charlotte Edwards, the captain of defending champions England, is a good example; as you can see in this footage of the 2012 Women's Twenty20 World Cup final, she manages to score quick runs with little reliance on brute force. In all likelihood, most of the ODI matches during WWC13 will be more exhibitions of skill than of power.

West Indies may have lost the opener after a strong performance by hosts India, for which many players stood out. But Dottin's 16-ball cameo in West Indies' chase threatened to overshadow it all. She smashed 39 runs, including three fours and four sixes, which is an astonishingly fast rate of scoring. This followed her three wickets with the ball, suggesting her quality as an aggressive all-rounder in the mould of Chris Cairns or Shahid Afridi from the men's game.

The most fearsome thing is that Dottin, who has also scored the fastest century in women's international Twenty20 cricket, is still only 21. Those strong forearms and shoulders could get even stronger, and the hits even bigger. And the women's game becomes a more varied spectacle all the time.

Women's Cricket World Cup 2013: No Semi-Finals?

Tuesday January 29, 2013

As far as I can tell, the Women's Cricket World Cup 2013 (WWC13) is unique among recent international cricket tournaments in that it has no semi-final stage.

(What? No semi-finals? But it's a world cup! All world cups have semi-finals! That's how they work!)

Well, that isn't how this World Cup works. Following the initial group stage, a Super Sixes round will result in team rankings for the tournament. The top two teams will play each other in the final, the next two will play the third place playoff, and so on. (Learn more about the Women's Cricket World Cup tournament format here.)

Is There Too Much Cricket Nowadays?

Sunday January 20, 2013

There's been a lot of discussion lately about Australia's policy of resting key players during the current one-day international series against Sri Lanka. Here's the rough story so far:

  • Australia's selectors rest captain Michael Clarke, Matthew Wade, and David Warner for the first two matches of the series.
  • Stand-in captain George Bailey responds with frustration to accusations that this is an Australian 'B' team.
  • Former star player Brett Lee, among many others, questions the selectors' rotation policy.
  • Mickey Arthur, Australia's coach, slams critics of the rotation system.
  • And captain Clarke defends the system too, saying Australia's top players need to be looked after.

Whatever you think about the sanctity of a place in an international cricket team, Clarke's point is a salient one. ESPNcricinfo's Brydon Coverdale wrote a thoughtful defence of player rotation here, noting that David Warner played 49 consecutive cricket matches for Australia in 2012.

If you extend the scope to all international cricket, a look at the international calendar for 2013 (January-June, July-December), it's plain to see that there's a LOT of cricket being played nowadays. A possible 48 matches are currently scheduled for Australia in 2013, including 15 five-day Test matches. The only real break in the international calendar is for the Champions League Twenty20, which will involve a number of top players as well. There's also the heavily commercialised Indian Premier League (IPL}, which is where many of the world's cricket stars earn their biggest paycheck of the year.

With all that in mind, it's reasonable for the Australian selection and management team to say yes, there is too much cricket for international players nowadays. Their controversial rotation policy has merit. As for the public, whether or not there is too much cricket is subjective; some miss the days of a more limited schedule, while others will always want more.

As for the cricket itself, Australia won the first match but were then hammered in the next two - including an embarrassing 74 all out in Brisbane. Well, every new innovation goes through some teething problems.
