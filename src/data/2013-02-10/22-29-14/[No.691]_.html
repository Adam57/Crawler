Resume' Nightmares

Thursday January 24, 2013

Anyone who comes into a civil engineering office for an interview (or at least, my office!) should really be aware of the fact that I'm going to test you on the skills you claim to have on your resume'. If you tell me you have experience using Map 3D to do land planning, I'm going to sit you down at a computer and make you show me. I am always amazed at how many people flat out lie on resume's and are shocked and insulted when I call them on it. I once had a guy come in, who claimed years of civil design experience, and when I asked how he would go about building a simple surface he said: "I can't do that, but I know where to look it up in my book from school." SMH - you can't make this stuff up. Simple advice, people, DO NOT lie on your resume'.

Image Designs

Wednesday January 23, 2013

You know what I'm seeing a lot of recently? Designs that are bypassing formal surveys and drafting in favor of using overlay imagery for their background information. I can see the benefit here, it's quick, cheap, and easy to do and I've done it myself on preliminary designs. Images are good enough for quick layouts and concepts, particularly with the latest tools for editing raster images in CAD. My concern is that I've seen some engineers using this process for actual designs. While I understand that full surveys aren't always required, I'm still nervous about using data that has such a large potential for discrepancy. Some aerial data is only accurate to within 10 ft. +/- Yikes! Basing designs off something like that seems dangerous to me. What do you think? Are you comfortable with images as part of your base design?

Today & Tomorrow

Friday January 18, 2013

Staying ahead of the development curve when you're responsible for all the design software in a large firm is no simple task. I make a point of keeping the latest version of all our software installed on my workstation for development and testing, even though my staff works one release behind. I also make sure to regularly visit the best beta and testing sites for our packages on a regular basis. Anyone out there who is working in any type of managerial position in the CAD world should be doing the same. How can you plan out how you're going to be working next year if you don't have access to those tools today? To handle that, I have two desktops (plus a laptop) that I use regularly. One is a standard install so I can support my users, and the other is my development system for testing what I'm going to roll out to my staff next.

What do you guys do for managing the present and the future of your CAD systems?
