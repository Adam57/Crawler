New Victor Reader Stream Adds Wi-Fi to DAISY Book & Media Player

Sunday February 3, 2013

It's hard not to like HumanWare's Victor Reader Stream. The DAISY book and media player stands out --at least to me -- as one the first assistive devices with cell phone sleekness.

The Stream's support for multiple accessible audio, text, and music file formats made it as close to a must-have as it gets for print-disabled readers, especially those with visual impairments. Intuitive navigation via keypad command makes files easy to store and access.

I still use my VRS Library Edition as a voice recorder, but find reading easier with the NLS Digital Talking Book Player and BARD downloads.

The new Stream is sleeker still: thinner and 28% smaller with rounded edges for a more comfortable grip. It also provides Wi-Fi connectivity to content providers such as Audible, Bookshare, and the National Library Service.

Unfortunately, mobile apps can replace these functions, enabling blind and visually people to carry one less device.

Still, the Stream is cheaper than an iPhone, and more immediately accessible. These strengths and continued enhancement should keep the product relevant for years to come.

Manage Email Via Voice Command with Talkler iOS App

Saturday February 2, 2013

Mobile app developers are finding more specialized uses for voice recognition that continue broaden the assistive technology's mainstream appeal.

A great example is Talkler -- a free iOS app for email management via voice command. Though designed for persons who are blind or visually impaired, hands-free email access also enables commuters to clear out their inbox on the way to work.

The app is easy to download and set up. It provides commands for reading, deleting, responding to, and managing email messages -- and even speaks command options, should you get stuck. Talkler is also compatible with VoiceOver, Apple's built-in screen reader.

It amazes me how far and fast this technology has come. Nuance Dragon NaturallySpeaking popularized speech recognition; Siri, the iPhone 4 assistant, made it ubiquitous, yet chic.

Now, it's just another add-on whose origins in disability are all but forgotten.

ZoomReader iOS App Makes Text More Accessible to Visually Impaired People On the Go

Tuesday January 29, 2013

One app can now combine assistive technologies that once required purchasing multiple applications and peripherals.

ZoomReader, for example, gives visually impaired people access to magnification, OCR scanning, text-to-speech, voice recognition, and the ability to share files via email -- all in one iOS app that costs $19.99.

The app makes it easy to read what you need at all times. Can't see the phone number? Click a picture of the page on your iPhone, scan it, and let ZoomReader read it to you. The same goes for street signs, menus, bills, and more.

ZoomReader can increase independence and dignity for persons with low-vision. No longer having to ask for help removes much of the stress associated with venturing out.

The Most Significant Apex HumanWare Achieves with its BrailleNote Devices is Price

Tuesday January 22, 2013

The BrailleNote Apex BT 32 braille notetaker provides wireless access to the web and connects to peripherals such as printers, braille embossers, and flash drives. It also serves as a braille terminal through which blind and visually impaired computer users can access and navigate their PC.

It's essentially a shell laptop: a braille keyboard and refreshable display bolstered by basic digital communications and web functions.

Would you spend $5,495 for such a device? Most blind people can't.

For braille literacy to reverse its decades-long decline, we need an innovative device that makes braille accessible and portable -- and priced so blind professionals can afford them and businesses can't call it an unreasonable accommodation.
