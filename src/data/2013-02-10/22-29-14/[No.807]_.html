Cod Limit Slashed 77 Percent

Friday February 8, 2013
    NEFMC Kills Fishing Singlehandedly
    The New England Fishery Management Council has cut the Gulf of Maine cod fishery limits by 77 percent. The radical action will be implemented for this year, as well as 2014 and 2015. The council voted to cut the cod allocation for the Georges Bank grounds by 66 percent.
    The action is a death blow to fishermen in the Northeast.
    In a press announcement last week, council members said revenues from all Northeastern ports dropping 33 percent. Fishermen believe the revenues will plummet to pennies. Here's why: the alottment of 1,550 metric tons - although larger than the minimum of 1,250 mt - is about the same amount that just New Hampshire boats bring in per year. Now share that among the entire Northeast.
    Fishermen are naturally frustrated, with many saying they won't even leave the docks this year, not as a threat, but for the simple reaso that they can't afford to. This has happened, by the way, despite 3 years of catch shares. Fishermen also see the return of seals and herring as playing as a factor in the decline of the cod population.
    Last year, regulators took emergency action to keep the annual cut to 22 percent. This year, they say the situation is too dire. And just to kick fishermen while they are down, vessels must now pay half the cost of at-sea monitors.
    Richard Gaines of The Gloucester Times reported the following, which is disturbing:
    "(With) Gulf of Maine cod, there's not enough to sustain the fishery. The game is over," said Vito Giacalone, policy director for the Northeast Seafood Coalition. He added that it was "tough to support a motion that says the best we've got is to collapse the fishery on the heels of a disaster."
    Councilor David Goethel, a New Hampshire groundfisherman, got no support for his motion to do just that -- shut down the entire directed groundfishery. Goethel argued that it made more sense to shut it down and dramatize the hardship in hope that NOAA and Congress recognized the crisis with disaster relief and the need to rethink the science and management of the fishery. He said a dramatic act that spared no one was preferable to "throwing 90 percent of the fishermen under the bus."

Louisiana Crawfish Rebounds

Sunday February 3, 2013
    Northern shrimp season may finish quickly
    The last two years for Louisiana's crawfish industry has been difficult. From the BP oil spill to meager years caused by summer drought and winter cold, it has been a long haul. But last year's wet summer and a relatively warm and wet winter this year are boosting the early crop for a season that normally peaks in late spring, in April and May.
    According to a report by the Associated Press, dealers are buying 150 to 200 sacks a day as opposed to the 30 or 40 they were buying last year. This has caused prices to go from last year's $3.50 to $4 per pound to $1.50 to $1.75.
    The previous year's weather plays a big part in every crawfish season because females seal themselves into underground burrows in May and stay there, eating nothing, until the first heavy rain after their eggs hatch in the fall. But they die if their burrows dry out. A dry burrow means the eggs, which are carried in a mass under the tail, won't set properly.
    If there's water for a successful hatch, the babies stay under the mother's tail until she removes the clay seal she created and comes out of her hole. Lack of rain will keep a female underground until November, by which time only 25 percent are alive.
    But the 2011 farm crop was the biggest and most lucrative in a decade, at 111.9 million pounds and $195.8 million dollars, according to the AgCenter's annual reports. However, the harvest per acre was the sixth-lowest from 2002 to 2011.
    Up north, things are different. Maine's shrimp trawling season opened Jan. 23, and within days there were hints from the Department of Marine Resources DMR that it wouldn't last long. By Jan. 25, DMR Commissioner Patrick Keliher announced that trawling would be allowed between sunrise and 3 p.m. on Monday and Wednesday, the only two days of the week when boats are allowed to fish.
    Maine shrimp fishermen are already dealing with severe restrictions.In addition to the two-day fishing week, the trawling fleet is limited to landing no more than some 539 metric tons (1.19 million pounds) of Northern shrimp. The fishery will close when landing data indicates that 85 percent of that quota has been caught.

These Horseshoe Crabs Need Good Luck

Tuesday January 29, 2013
    Problems beyond Hurricane Sandy.
    State officials and environmental advocates are at odds with the situation concerning New Jersey, horseshoe crabs, habitat, Hurricane Sandy and the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission
Because of Sandy, more than half of the crabs' breeding in the Delaware Bay have been destroyed. A state survey recently reported that Sandy destroyed at least 50 percent - maybe more - of the crab's egg-laying habitat along both shores of Delaware Bay. The habitat is an important habitat for migratory shorebirds.
    Now, in a seemingly contradictory move, State Sen. Jeff Van Drew has introduced a bill that would lift a 5-year-old ban on catching the crabs, initiated in 2008, thus returning the power on how many crabs can be harvested to the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission.
    Environmentalists believe the crab population has not rebounded at all since the moratorium was enacted and the last thing the stressed breeding population of the crab needs now is further fishing. The two sides rely on separate models and studies, which each have a different result. The Atlantic States council uses a Virginia Tech study that counts the crabs and shows the population in the Delaware Bay and elsewhere to be flat. A different study observing the population along the entire coast show a slight increase but no significant data from Delaware Bay.
    The moratorium cannot be lifted until a marked increase is observed, but since it seems like that's not happening, it appears he wants to make it the problem of the ASMFC. But more notably, the Garden State Seafood Association, says a new model that as many as 100,000 male horseshoe crabs could be taken per year from the bay without any negative effect.
    The senator has said that crabs are still being harvested on the other side of the bay on the Delaware side and the moratorium is hurting the state's fishermen.
    Horseshoe crabs have value as bait to certain types of fishermen, especially conch fishermen and also to the biomedical industry, which collects the crabs for the blood.

McSustainable

Sunday January 27, 2013
    Marine Stewardship Council & McDonald's
    On Jan. 24, 2013, McDonald's announced it will adopt the Marine Stewardship Council's blue eco-label on its fish packaging in restaurants across the country. That's a lot of fish.
    McDonald's is one of the largest single buyers of fish in the United States. The move shows the company's commitment to sustainable fishing, with all fish sold in its US stores to be certified sustainable. Believe it or not, they have been practicing sustainable fishing in various forms and extents for the last 10 years, with some stores in Europe already sustainable. McDonald's already uses MSC-certified wild Alaska pollock for its Filet-O-Fish sandwich, will begin displaying the MSC eco-label on product packaging, in-restaurant communications and external marketing beginning in February. At the same time, McDonald's will also launch Fish McBites, which also uses wild-caught, MSC-certified Alaska Pollock. I think of it as a fish McNugget.
    For the burger king (sorry), more fish and eco-friendly choices are part of its changing strategy to compete with more "nutritious", chic or "adult" rivals.
    MSC Chief Executive Rupert Howes said this event is the culmination of successes stemming from a working, collaborative relationship. MSC certification indicates that over 14,000 McDonald's restaurants across the U.S. have met the MSC Chain of Custody standard for traceability, which is the ability to track the fish all the way back through the supply chain to the fishery.
    Under the MSC program, these fisheries have been assessed by independent scientists against three core principles: the health of the fish stock, the impact of the fishery on the ecosystem and the management system that oversees the fishery.
    In 2011, McDonald's introduced MSC certification for all its European restaurants. McDonald's USA also worked with the Sustainable Fisheries Partnership and Conservation International for more than ten years to support sustainable fishing practices.
