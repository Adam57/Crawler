Motorcycle Engine Swaps

Wednesday February 6, 2013

It sounds easy: pull the old broken engine out and put a better one in, right? Unfortunately, that's the sort of thing we see all too often on cable TV shows -- you know, the ones that make everything sound easy. The reality of many of their projects is not quite the same.

It's easy to understand someone thinking about swapping an engine when he or she has just had a quote for replacement parts to repair the old one, but it is very important to do a lot of research first or you will simply swap one set of expensive problems for another set.

Classic owners are very familiar with the original story of the café racers of the 60s, and it was these machines that have encouraged many owners to try something similar. But it must be remembered that 60s motorcycles were relatively uncomplicated machines, especially when compared to bikes coming into the classic definition of being twenty five years old. (Putting a Bonneville engine into a Norton frame is one thing, putting a 1988 VF1000R engine into a GSXR frame is something altogether different!)

Painting Motorcycle Fenders

Monday February 4, 2013

Improving the appearance of a classic can be as simple as having a part re-chromed or as involved as a complete repaint. But for most owners, a typical appearance improvement job will be painting a few parts.

Three of the most visible parts on a classic are the fuel tank and the front and rear fenders. Getting a good finish on a fender, for instance, is relatively simple and can easily be done in a small workshop.

However, there is one essential element when painting a part: patience. The time between coats of paint, or the clear coats, must be as per the paint manufacturer's recommendations. This is especially true where multiple colors or pin stripes are to be added as this will invariably require placing masking tapes on new paint. Needless to say, the paint must be dry at this point.

Maintenance on Vintage and Classic Bikes

Saturday January 26, 2013

I'm quite sure the original designers of some of our current classics and vintage bikes would be amazed at the mileage the bikes have covered. Either that, or amazed at the fact these bikes are still around!

As these machines get older, their maintenance requirements change; they will still need to have all the usual service items attended to, but other items will need consideration too. For example, much of the exposed hardware (engine bolts, side stand pivot bolts, fender stay bolts, etc.) should be inspected and replaced if there are any signs of corrosion or if the threads are becoming worn.

When the age of a motorcycle surpasses typical total miles, many other items will need to be serviced or replaced. Unfortunately, every machine is different. But for the most part, a common sense approach is all that is needed for ongoing maintenance.

Adding Red-Kote™ to Gas Tanks

Thursday January 24, 2013

Many classic motorcycle tanks need to have the insides treated or sealed to stop rust from getting into the fuel system. Anyone who has experienced the nightmare of having to clean the carb/s and fuel system over and over, before realizing the tank was the problem, will testify that sealing inside a gas tank should be one of the first jobs when buying a classic motorcycle.

There are many good tank sealers on the market, but the top seller is Red-Kote™. However, it is imperative to follow the manufacturer's instructions to obtain good results. Attending to any loose rust inside the tank and applying the sealer within the correct temperature and humidity range are all must does.

Another important point, when sealing a tank, is when to do it? That is, should it be sealed before or after painting, for instance? As the sealer Red-Kote is designed to stick to surfaces, it is best to apply it as soon as possible. If you get it onto a freshly painted tank the paint can, and most likely will, be damaged. Taking care during the preparation phase, therefore, is a must.
