What Drew Us to Geoscience

Sunday February 10, 2013

Tim Wright is a geoscience professor at the University of Leeds. The other day he used his Twitter account, @timwright_leeds, to ask his followers what drew them into Earth science. He suggested that everyone replying use the hashtag #WhyEarthSci to make the responses easy to monitor, and as so often happens the whole thing spread through the community. My response—limited to 140 characters like every Twitter post—was, "#WhyEarthSci I couldn't shake a fascination with landscape and planets." Wright has harvested and collated the responses on the Storify site, where the fleeting strings of Twitter conversation can be preserved. See his "WhyEarthSci" story for a quick survey of our core turn-ons.

Geo-Quiz of the Week: Fossils

Saturday February 9, 2013

Not every geologist is a fossil expert, just as not every geologist is a volcanologist, petrologist, hydrologist, mining engineer, et cetera. But every geologist, no matter what their specialty is, gets asked about fossils. And many of us found our first fixation in fossils. Nevertheless, the Fossils Geo-Quiz is the kind of quiz where lay people have a fairly equal chance of matching the pros, because everyone knows a little something about fossils. Students: Should You Get Extra Credit?

Thursday February 7, 2013

Callan Bentley is a geology professor whose Mountain Beltway blog appears regularly on the AGU website. Today he asks, "What do you think of extra credit as an option in teaching?" Already he's gotten a wide range of responses from his fellow teachers. (One of his own teachers replied, "Did I ever offer extra credit? 'nough said.")

What about you, students? I hope you'll read the post and its responses for insight into your teachers' lives and work. What has extra credit done for you, and why should your teachers keep offering it? I have no stake in this, since I don't even remember back that far.

More Evidence for Infrasound-Sensing Birds

Wednesday February 6, 2013

The current issue of The Atlantic covers a recent paper on the hypothesis that homing pigeons find their way around the landscape by its soundscape. Jon Hagstrum, a geophysicist with the U.S. Geological Survey, has explored this line of research in his spare time for many years, and I featured his work here in 2008. The new paper, in the Journal of Experimental Biology, explores a mystifying day in August 1969 when a batch of homing pigeons, released by researchers at a chronically "dead" hilltop in upstate New York, didn't get lost for once. (This was two days before Woodstock, by the way.) Hagstrum used weather data from that day to show that the "infrasound landscape" was shifted. Just as sometimes the atmosphere lets you listen to a distant radio station, on that day the birds could feel the right way home.

The Atlantic story, by Rebecca Rosen, is well done. So is a commentary on the paper appearing in the same issue of the journal, by Kathryn Knight.
