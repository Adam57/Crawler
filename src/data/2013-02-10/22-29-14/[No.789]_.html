Smart snacks in school

Monday February 4, 2013

Last Friday, the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) proposed new standards for competitive foods at school--those sold at snack bars, in vending machines and so on. The Healthy, Hunger-Free Kids Act required the USDA to establish nutrition standards for all foods sold in schools (not just school lunches and breakfasts). The "Smart Snacks in School" proposal is the first step toward creating those nutrition guidelines. The USDA reports these highlights:

  • More of the foods we should encourage (schools should promote healthy snack foods with whole grains, low fat dairy, fruits, vegetables or protein foods as their main ingredients).
  • Less of the foods we should avoid (snack foods should be lower in fat, sugar, and sodium).
  • Flexibility for important traditions. (parents can still send in bagged lunches, and treats for birthday parties and other celebrations; and schools can continue traditions like fundraisers and bake sales).
  • Reasonable limitations on when and where the standards apply (standards only affect foods that are sold on school campus during the school day, not after school).
  • Flexibility for state and local communities (these rules are minimum requirements only; states and schools that have stronger standards will be able to maintain their own policies).

You can see the rule and make comments on it for the next 60 days. As many as 39 states already have laws or policies in place about snack foods and beverages in schools; the USDA standards are meant to set a "national baseline." Research has shown that strong, specific, and consistent rules about competitive foods may help kids avoid weight gain. Let's hope these new standards can make a difference.

Meet a Super Kid

Wednesday January 30, 2013

At Sunday night's Super Bowl, keep an eye out for 10-year-old Hunter Paulin. As 2013's NFL Play 60 Super Kid, he will present the game ball to an official right before the game begins.

Hunter is a Washington Redskins fan who was born with some heart defects. That means he has to be creative about how he exercises, but he still loves to be active with his family.

Hunter was one of thousands of kids who entered the NFL PLAY 60 Super Bowl Contest, sponsored by the NFL and Quaker Oats. To be eligible to win, Hunter pledged to be active for 60 minutes a day. He also answered questions about his commitment to being healthy, how NFL players inspire him to be fit (his favorite is Redskins quarterback Robert Griffin III), how his family stays active and healthy and how eating right helps to power his play.

Year-round, NFL PLAY 60 challenges kids to get active for 60 minutes a day. Visit NFLRUSH.com for tips from NFL pros and ways kids can help their community be more active.
