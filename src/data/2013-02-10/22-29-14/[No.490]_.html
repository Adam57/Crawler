African-American History Month 2013: Celebrating Freedom and Activism

Friday February 1, 2013

When Carter G. Woodson established Negro History Week in 1926, his mission was simple: he wanted to honor the efforts of men such as Abraham Lincoln and Frederick Douglas, who both worked to emancipate African-Americans. Since then, Negro History Week has been expanded to the entire month of February.

Every year the Association for the Study of African American Life and History establishes a theme for African-American History Month. This year's theme commemorates two important events in African-American History: the 150th Anniversary of the Emancipation Proclamation and the 50th Anniversary of the March on Washington .

During this month, I'll continue to honor the legacy of so many African-American men and women who risked thier lives to create change in the United States. In addition, I'll be highlighting African-Americans who played a pivotal role in the emancipation of enslaved people and fighting for civil rights in the United States.

Georgia Douglas Johnson

Thursday January 31, 2013
When Georgia Douglas Johnson published her first volume of poetry, she was thirty-six years old. Although Johnson started her career as a writer later than women such as Alice Dunbar Nelson , her work was prolific.

In addition, Johnson's ability to run a literary salon--providing a space for writers and artists to thrive during the Harlem Renaissance was important to its existence.

Alice Dunbar-Nelson

Thursday January 31, 2013
Poet, journalist and political activistAlice Dunbar-Nelson once wrote in her dairy "Damn bad luck I have with my pen. Some fate has decreed I shall never make money by it." Although Dunbar-Nelson achieved acclaim, working as an African-American woman during the Progressive Era and the Harlem Renaissance.

Yet, her work as a poet and journalist are respected today for their ability to explore varied themes of African-American life.

Toni Morrison

Thursday January 31, 2013
Novelist Toni Morrison has received national acclaim for books such as The Bluest Eye, Song of Solomon, and Beloved. Morrison often credits the works of William Faulkner, Jane Austen and Virginia Wolf for helping her develop complex characters, settings and plots. Yet I discovered while writing that she admired and was deeply encouraged by James Baldwin. In 1987, Morrison wrote an essay in The New York Times Book Review honoring Baldwin's work as a writer.
