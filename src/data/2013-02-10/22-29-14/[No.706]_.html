Post Office Eliminates Saturday Delivery; May Affect Credit Card Payments

Thursday February 7, 2013

Starting in August, the post office will cut Saturday mail deliveries to homes and businesses. You'll have to take extra steps to be sure your credit card or loan payments won't be late.

If your due date falls on a Saturday (or even Sunday) you'll need to mail your payments a little earlier to ensure the credit card issuer receives the payment on time. It may require a little extra effort, but it'll help you avoid a late fee. Alternatively, you can make a payment online or by phone with no fee as long as it's in advance of your due date. Your creditor can charge a fee to expedite a payment made on the due date.

New to online bill pay? Here's an overview of paying your credit card bill online.

Use a bill pay checklist to help track your payment due dates each month. Or pen your bills on a printable calendar (a few months in advance is even better) to help keep track of your due dates.

Barclaycard Rewards MasterCard for Excellent Credit - $50 Bonus

Monday February 4, 2013

If you have excellent credit - upper 700s and above - consider the Barclaycard Rewards MasterCard for Excellent Credit. You'll earn 5,000 points after your first purchase or balance transfer, worth $50 in cashback.

With the "excellent credit" version of the Barclaycard Rewards MasterCard, cardholders enjoy 0% interest on purchases and balance transfers for 15 months and double points on gas, groceries, and utilities.

There's also a version of the card for good credit. You can still earn rewards at the same rate and you get a 2,500 bonus after the first purchase. The introductory rate lasts 6 months.

Neither card requires a minimum purchase amount to receive your bonus. The only requirement is that you don't return the purchase after you've received the bonus.

Understand Your Card's Promotional Rate

Thursday January 31, 2013

Some of the most attractive credit card deals are the ones with 0% APR promotional rates. Some credit card issuers offer the promotional rates on balance transfers only and others apply the same low rate to purchases and balance transfers.

As the saying goes, all good things must some to an end. Introductory rates only last a certain amount of time, at least six months, but sometimes longer. If you sign up for a credit card with a 0% rate, it's important that you know when the rate ends, especially if you sign up for a card that backdates interest if the balance isn't paid by the end of the promotional period.

Before you take advantage of a 0% or other low introductory offer, make sure you understand how promotional rates work. Otherwise, the promotion may not be beneficial.

Here are a few credit cards with great promotional rates:

  • Discover it Card - 0% on purchases and transfers for 14 months
  • Citi Diamond Preferred Card - 0% on purchases and balance transfers for 18 months
  • Barclaycard Rewards MasterCard - 0% on purchases and balance transfers for 15 months

Keep in mind that offers are subject to approval and approval is based on your credit history, income, debt, and other factors. People with excellent credit typically have a better chance at qualifying for the best rates. Credit card issuers also change their offers, so make sure you review their site for the current pricing information.

Credit Card Purchases May Come With a Fee

Monday January 28, 2013

Soon, we may have to pay a fee for credit card transactions. The change is a result of a lawsuit settlement between several retailers and Visa, MasterCard, and a few other banks.

For years, merchants have paid surcharge fees between 1.5% and 3% of each transaction to credit card issuers and processing networks like Visa and MasterCard. If they choose, merchants can pass some or all of this cost to consumers.

The terms of the settlement requires retailers who charge for credit card transactions to disclose the fee in several places - at store entry, at the register, and on the receipt - so consumers, hopefully, won't be surprised by the fee. In addition, merchants are prohibited from charging excessive fees. The fees don't apply to debit card transactions, as long as you use your check card as a debit card.

Ten states have laws that prohibit merchants from charging a fee on credit card purchases. If you live in one of these states, you can report any point-of-sale credit card fees to your state Attorney General: California, Colorado, Connecticut, Florida, Kansas, Maine, Massachusetts, New York, Oklahoma, and Texas.

Pay attention to posted notices at the places you shop and if you're in doubt, ask the cashier whether there's a charge for credit card transactions.
