    By Eric Holthaus

    PJ Smith for The Wall Street Journal
    Friday’s blizzard turned the Forest Park golf course as a popular spot for sledding.

    While the historic nature of this weekend’s blizzard is still settling in across the tri-state, the first in a string of additional storms will arrive Monday.

    As a (comparatively smaller) blizzard works its way eastward from the midwest on Monday, warm air will be drawn northward into Greater New York. Temperatures should easily top 40 degrees in New York City. Further south, where there was hardly any snow, temperatures will reach the 50s (possibly even the low 60s) over parts of southern New Jersey. In areas with the deepest lingering snowpack (like Connecticut), temperatures will be kept much cooler.

    The result will be a complex forecast. Here’s what may happen: snow flurries or light sleet could fall in colder areas Monday morning and again overnight Monday, but the rest of the region (including New York City) will be entirely rain throughout. The biggest problem will be the repeated thaw/freeze cycles over the next couple of nights that could make walking and driving extremely slippery. If temperatures get too warm over Long Island, for example, some flooding could occur as slush clogs drains and snow quickly melts. And there is a lot of snow out there to melt: about four inches of water worth, if it all melted instantly.

    Forecasts for a glaze of ice on roadways have prompted the National Weather Service to issue a freezing rain advisory for Monday morning across most of the tri-state, not including New York City. Concern is also growing that the sporadic roof collapses seen across Long Island and Connecticut on Sunday could accelerate with the added weight of Monday’s rain.

    After Monday’s rain, temperatures warm enough for daytime melting should continue across the entire region. Monday should be the warmest day this week, though.

    By Thursday — Valentine’s Day — another snow producer could be approaching the tri-state. Right now models are wavering on whether or not the storm system comes close enough to the coast to cause snowfall locally, but there’s still enough of a chance that it’s worth mentioning. It’s way too early to say how much snow could fall if the storm does materialize.

    By the weekend, a bigger storm than Wednesday’s is potentially looming. The ECMWF model (the one that’s been performing the best over the last several months) has consistently been hinting at this possibility for a couple of days. Again, it’s too early to say how much snow could fall, but the pattern would support the entire tri-state getting additional accumulation.

    Just where does this blizzard rank in history?
    The Weather Underground’s weather historian, Christopher Burt has an excellent analysis of exactly where the just-completed epic blizzard ranks in the litany of major New England winter storms, stretching back centuries. His conclusion: the latest blizzard is probably the second worst in the last 300 years in Connecticut and Long Island, topped only by the Great Blizzard of 1888.

    He adds that it’s “a bit unsettling” that two of the region’s most intense storms in centuries — the blizzard and Sandy — have occurred within only a few months of each other. To that, I’d add that New York City — as well as the region and country as a whole — have also just completed their warmest years on record (2012). The juxtaposition of these startling events can also be scientifically linked. As an example, there’s ample evidence that climate change is causing the most extreme weather events to create more rain — and more snow.

    Weather Journal’s Greater New York forecast roundup for Monday:

    • WSJ Headquarters in Manhattan:
    • Actual High: 45; High feels like: 41
    • Actual Low: 36; Low feels like: 33
    • Weather: Evening rain could turn icy
    • Brooklyn: 45/35
    • Queens: 45/36
    • Bronx: 45/36
    • Staten Island: 46/35
    • Poughkeepsie, N.Y.: 45/31
    • Trenton, N.J.: 52/35
    • Islip, Long Island: 45/35
    • New Haven, Conn.: 45/33

    Meteorologist Eric Holthaus contributes daily weather reports and analysis on Metropolis. For the latest on conditions in New York and elsewhere, follow his updates (@EricHolthaus) on Twitter.
