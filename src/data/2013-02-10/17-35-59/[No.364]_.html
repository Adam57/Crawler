    Is AOL Trimming Its Patch? Year-End Goal Now In Doubt Jeff Bercovici Forbes Staff

    Tim Armstrong. (Image credit: Getty Images North America via @daylife)

    Here’s the good news: AOL managed to boost its total revenues on a year-over-year basis in the fourth quarter of 2012, the first time in eight years that’s happened. And it wasn’t a close-run thing, either. Revenues were up $25 million, to $599 million.

    Now here’s the bad news: Patch, the hyperlocal news network that makes up a large part of AOL’s content business, fell substantially short of its revenue target for the year. Six months ago, CEO Tim Armstrong said Patch’s 900 or so sites would take in $40 million to $50 million in 2012. The actual total came in at $34 million.

    On an earnings call with analysts, Armstrong said Hurricane Sandy, which affected more than a third of the towns in Patch’s network, had a lot to do with the shortfall. “Sandy essentially followed the path of the Patch rollouts,” he said.

    He also said that Patch ended up placing more emphasis on cost-reduction, in a successful effort to bring down expenses that at one point reached $40 million per quarter, than on ad sales. He declined to say how much Patch lost in 2012.

    The revenue miss comes as Patch is approaching crunch time. For the past couple years, Armstrong has been promising that the network will achieve run-rate profitability by the fourth quarter of 2013.

    That’s still the case, Armstrong said. “We are committed to getting to Q4 profitability as a business,” he said. “That remains our goal, our intent we’re moving toward it.”

    He suggested that Patch can make up a lot of ground quickly by courting regional advertisers, a group it has largely overlooked in favor of local and national, and by partnering with metropolitan newspapers and television stations that have cut back coverage of their surrounding suburbs.

    And there’s still more wiggle room on the cost side. AOL will soon be rolling out “a simplified version of Patch coming which essentially focuses on three or four things,” Armstrong said.

    All those initiatives sound promising enough. But if AOL can break one promise on Patch, why not another? And it’s not the first time the company has pulled back from an ambitious target for Patch, either.

    Meanwhile, other big media conglomerates are reconsidering the size of the hyperlocal opportunity. This week, NBC shut down its own entrant, Everyblock.

    In short, there are reasons to be bullish about AOL’s prospects, but optimism about Patch is not one of them.
