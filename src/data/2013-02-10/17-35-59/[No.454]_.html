Stargazing: New York Fashion Week Days 2 and 3

By BEE-SHYUAN CHANG
Ben Gabbe/Getty ImagesThe front row at the Rag & Bone show from left: Grace Coddington, Hamish Bowles and Drew Barrymore.

Forget the Postal Service (especially after it announced that it would eliminate Saturday service later this year): When it comes to sleet and snow, nothing keeps the fashion crowd from its appointed rounds.

Even if most insist on ignoring the elements when it comes to their chosen attire for Fashion Week.

On Saturday morning at Lincoln Center, among the expected parkas and knee-high boots, were a surprising number of stilettos in the front row at the Ruffian show. “Who are these girls that are wearing open-toed shoes?” asked the fashion reporter Robert Verdi, layered all in black with gray Hunter rain boots. “It’s frustrating just to see them,” he added. “What, did they think those heels were ice picks?”

New York Fashion Week

Coverage from in and around the New York shows.

The day before, at the Jason Wu show on Park Avenue, Fashion Week veterans came prepared. “I have my snow boots in a backpack in the corner,” said Elettra Wiedemann, who was in a swingy skirt, no hose and strappy pumps. (Other celebrity guests included Jamie King and Allison Williams, and the model Stephanie Seymour, along with her two sons, the ubiquitous Brant Boys). “And really it’s just about having a good car service,” she said matter-of-factly before floating away to socialize with Alina Cho of CNN.

At the Friday night Rag & Bone show, amid a sea of stiletto pumps (and a crowd that included Drew Barrymore in a camel wide-brimmed hat), the designer Kenneth Cole, who was off duty for the night, observed, referring to the snowstorm by name: “This weather is crazy, but it’s going to take more than Nemo to separate a woman from her heels.” (Video: Fashion Week Weather Woes)

The cheerful mood was bolstered by some brilliant details: Rag & Bone logo handwarmers, mulled wine and hot pretzels kept even cranky editors happy.

Ms. Barrymore’s verdict on the runway designs? “My favorite was the pops of orange: the color was done so well,” she said after the last look. “And the real genius is that you can wear everything. It’s not like those fashion shows where you wonder, ‘How can I wear that in my real life without looking like Phyllis Diller?’ ”

As the weekend progressed, the shows went on largely as scheduled, and celebrities turned out in better-than-expected numbers.

At Lacoste on Saturday morning, few photographers recognized Brad Smith, a wide receiver for the Buffalo Bills who had also attended the Tommy Hilfiger men’s show, along with the Super Bowl most valuable player, Joe Flacco, the night before.

“N.F.L. players are caring more about what they wear,” Mr. Smith said, although he admitted it was probably easier for him and Mr. Flacco. “We don’t have the proportions of a 350-pound lineman, but we do have longer arms,” he said. He was wearing Lacoste for the show, of course, but usually you’d find him in Vince, his favorite label. “They use good materials,” he said.

At the Prabal Gurung show in SoHo, one prime bench was especially jammed with glossy beauties. There was Ms. Wiedemann and Ms. King again, sandwiched with Analeigh Tipton. Perhaps more intriguing was the actress andrapper Eve, whose red and crystal-encrusted nails gleamed in the low lighting. “I only do it every so often,” she said of nail art. “But I just did a music video. It’s been a long time since I’ve been back in music.” She has a new album out May 14, she said, including a single with the peppy Cobra Starship. “We’re a very odd couple,” said Eve, chuckling. “But I think he went out of his comfort zone.”

Back at Lincoln Center for Mara Hoffman and Hervé Leger, pretty girls in pretty dresses (and Nigel Barker) attracted all the attention. Somewhere between Miss USA 2011, Miss Teen USA and Miss USA 2013, they all blurred together. Chrissie Morris, at Ms. Hoffman’s show, stood out. She had been working on her YouTube interview series that runs on Pharrell’s network, she said.

“I like YouTube because you can swear,” Ms. Morris explained, laughing. “I’ve actually had some people approach me to do some TV stuff, and it freaked me out.”

The highlight of the afternoon was, predictably, the Alexander Wang show, with his recent appointment as the new designer for Balenciaga heightening interest in what he would show under his own label. There were the usual Wang regulars like Terry Richardson, Zoe Kravitz and Liberty Ross, who had walked in the show last season after the Kristen Stewart-Rupert Sanders scandal.

“That was such a good moment for me,” said Ms. Ross, who was sitting front row this time around. “In retrospect, I gave it a lot of thought. It was the timing and also I hadn’t walked in a show in New York before.”

Ms. Ross, who recently filed for divorce from Mr. Sanders, was visibly energized. “I’m the happiest I’ve ever been,” she said.

Josh Haner/The New York TimesTyson Chandler of the New York Knicks with his wife Kimberly Chandler at the Alexander Wang show.

Across the runway, Tyson Chandler and his wife, Kimberly Chandler, were also in a good mood. Mr. Chandler’s team, the New York Knicks, has been winning. “We just want to bring a championship to the city,” said the center, dressed in a black Givenchy zip top, YSL shoes and 3X1 jeans. The inseam on those bad boys? “I actually have no idea,” he said “I’ve been doing custom a long time, but I bet they’re something crazy, like 40 inches.” (The couple also showed at the Altuzarra show later that evening, sharing the front row with Anna Wintour and Ms. Cho.)

After the runway show, ASAP Rocky, who also attended last season, gave his two cents. “It was like the wilderness,” he said of the clothes. “It’s like what I do as an artist– tap into my childhood –like those furry boxing gloves he had.”

If his star has exploded since the last runway show, he seemed collected. “I’m normal; I’m just chillin’,” he said. Normal? “Yeah, you got me confused with that other rapper – that’s 2 Chainz,” he joked, gold grill flashing in an impish grin.

“I’m different,” he said.
