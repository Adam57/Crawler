Outing NATO commander optimistic Afghanistan winning against Taliban

Michael Edwards reported this story on Monday, February 11, 2013 06:11:00

Listen to MP3 of this story ( minutes)

Alternate WMA version | MP3 download

TONY EASTLEY: The outgoing commander of NATO forces in Afghanistan says the Western-backed government there is on the road to winning the war against the Taliban.

General John Allen handed over control of international forces to his successor General Joseph Dunford in Kabul on Sunday.

As General Allen departs the White House still won't confirm how many troops will remain in the country at the end of Western withdrawal in 2014 and many analysts don't share his optimism over the future of the country.

South Asia correspondent Michael Edwards:

(Music)

MICHAEL EDWARDS: In a ceremony in Kabul, General John Allen ended his tenure as the head of Western forces in Afghanistan.

The departing commander painted a positive picture of the situation in the war-torn country.

JOHN ALLEN: Afghan forces defending Afghan people and enabling the government of this country to serve its citizens. This is victory. This is what winning looks like and we should not shrink from using these words.

MICHAEL EDWARDS: General Allen's key task during his 19-month tour of duty has been to oversee the transfer of security from Western forces to the Afghan army ahead of the 2014 withdrawal.

This has coincided with a sharp rise in green on blue attacks, with more than 60 Western troops killed by supposed Afghan allies in 2012.

In an interview with the BBC General Allen described Afghanistan's military forces as capable. But he urged patience when it comes to early assessments of their performance.

JOHN ALLEN: We need to be patient as we have in other counter-insurgencies and be ready to assist over the long term to build and assist the Afghans to build those institutions of governance and establishment of the rule of law, which then can create opportunities for economic development. That is what ultimately creates long term security.

MICHAEL EDWARDS: In a potentially troubling sign Afghanistan's president Hamid Karzai was absent from the transfer of command ceremony. And many analysts do not share the optimistic tone of General Allen's departing remarks.

Heather Barr is based in Kabul for Human Rights Watch.

HEATHER BARR: I think that it's the kind of very optimistic statement that we've been hearing from the military here for years, which don't necessarily have that much to do with what's happening on the ground.

I think a more honest statement is what a former US ambassador to Afghanistan said recently. He said, "We're not winding down the damn war, we're winding down our participation in [it, and] that's something extraordinarily different."

And I think that's a very honest way of looking at the situation. The war will continue, but the international troops won't be part of it.

MICHAEL EDWARDS: And according to Heather Barr, pessimism over Afghanistan's future is swelling the ranks of those leaving the country.

HEATHER BARR: Well I think one of the strongest indications you can see about the feeling on the ground is that last year 50,000 Afghans left, which is a record number for recent years - people fleeing to North America, people fleeing to Europe. And that doesn't even count people going to Pakistan or Iran. So I think that we're seeing Afghans in significant numbers voting with their feet.

MICHAEL EDWARDS: The nature of US military involvement after the official withdrawal in 2014 is yet to be decided by the White House.

The job of overseeing the last of the withdrawal will be up to General Allen's successor General Joseph Dunford.

This is Michael Edwards reporting for AM.
