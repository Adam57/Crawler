New report on Paterno, Penn State lends little clarity

Mike Lopresti, USA TODAY Sports1:53p.m. EST February 10, 2013

Lopresti: Paterno family's response to Freeh investigation and its conclusions only rearranges rubble at Penn State

Like the whereabouts and future of this statue of Joe Paterno that was taken down from outside Beaver Stadium shortly after the Freeh report was released, there are many questions unresolved about the late coach's legacy.(Photo: Gene J. Puskar, AP)

Story Highlights

  • Critique commissioned by late coach's family not likely to sway opinion on his legacy
  • All the reports in the world should never shift focus from the real victims, the children
  • Memory of Paterno likely to remain forever cast in shadows

Joe Paterno? It'd be nice to give you a confident verdict, a conclusion without clouds. Can't be done. Not now, and maybe not ever.

We'll never really know. That was one of the messages Sunday from the latest report that was dropped upon the national conscience. There is the unsteady and uncertain foundation on which his legacy will likely forever stand.

Another exposè, another re-shoveling of the debris at Penn State. Here's a guess at the impact.

Those of you who heap the blame on Paterno still do.

Those of you who intend to stand by him forever are even more determined never to leave his side.

As for anyone still waiting and hoping for the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth, we'll never get it. Who do you ask?

FAMILY ANSWERS: Paternos challenge Freeh's findings

This is a long, tragic movie that will always want for a neat ending, and you can forget a happy one. Just questions never answered. What we'll always know is where the compassion should start, because we'll always know who the real victims were.

Not a dozen reports will ever change where the No. 1 focus of this story should be.

But when it comes to the how and the why and the who, the reports will slant this way or that way, giving the world pieces of what happened.

There will be presumptions sometimes packaged as facts, assumptions decorated as evidence. Who wants to make the final call on a man's life with that? I don't.

That is why the Jerry Sandusky matter is appropriately settled with a prison term into eternity, but the rightful legacy of Joe Paterno is very much an unclosed case. You can revile him at one end or worship him on the other, but the real deal has to be somewhere in between.

The latest salvo to salvage the Paterno name was released in 238 pages, commissioned by his family. It is slightly shorter than the Freeh report but is no less relentless in the attack on its intended target. Both were delivered by accomplished men with respected pasts. It would be impossible to brand either inept or dishonest.

Freeh raised troubling issues. So did this one. Freeh identified the culprit. Same as this one.

Freeh could not be believed in its entirety. You wondered about perspective and context, no matter the expertise of the author. Same for this one. The Paterno family paid for it. Everyone knew what was coming.

Each report had its absorbing details. Sunday's report tells us that the last written words of Paterno's life were to express a thought that the ``good side'' of the scandal was giving the issue of child sexual abuse a full national airing.

When the Freeh report came out, I asked myself this awful question.

What if it's true? Even part of it?

If so, a legend had been vaporized. A man who had purportedly stood for something higher had had his name muddied forever. And rightfully so. He had aided and abetted villainy.

Now that this report is out, I ask myself this awful question.

What if it's true? Even part of it?

If so, a man's entire life was defiled by haste and a howling mob.

The title used is phrased ``The Rush to Injustice Regarding Joe Paterno.''

There is no clear answer about the ``injustice'' part. That is, and probably always will be, a matter of who each of us believes.

But there is certainly merit to the word ``rush.'' Especially the NCAA, not with the punishment handed Penn State, but by the manner in which it was decided. Due process was trampled, and that is not how a governing body is supposed to behave.

If the Paterno family expects a dramatic change in public opinion from Sunday's news, it will be disappointed. There will never be full redemption, as there will never be full condemnation. The memory of Joe Paterno remains in the shadows, with both light and darkness, probably for all time. We'll never really know.
